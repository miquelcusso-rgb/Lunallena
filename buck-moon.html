The Buck Moon

July's full moon — named for the new antlers male deer grow each summer, not its colour. When it peaks, why it rides low and golden, and how to catch it at moonrise.

Short answer
The Buck Moon is July's full moon. In 2026 it peaks on July 29 at 14:36 UTC — during daylight over the Americas, so it's best seen the nights of July 29 and 30. It's named for the velvet antlers male deer regrow in early summer in Native American tradition, not its colour — though as one of the lowest full moons of the year it often glows amber near the horizon.

Why "Buck"?

The name comes from the Algonquin peoples and other Native American traditions. Early summer is when male deer — bucks — push out their new antlers, which they shed and regrow every single year; by July the antlers are growing fast in soft velvet. June's full moon marked the wild strawberries; July's marked the bucks. The same moon has also been called the Thunder Moon, for July's frequent storms, the Hay Moon, for the haymaking season, and the Mead Moon.

Why it rides low and golden

July's full moon falls just after the summer solstice, so — like June's Strawberry Moon — it sits opposite a high Sun and takes one of the lowest paths of the year across the southern sky. A low Moon shines through far more atmosphere, which scatters away blue wavelengths and leaves a warm amber-gold tint, especially near the horizon. Add the "moon illusion" — our brain perceives a horizon Moon as larger — and the Buck Moon often looks unusually big and golden just as it rises.

Frequently asked questions

Does the Buck Moon look different from other full moons?
Physically it's identical to any full moon. But because July's full moon is one of the lowest of the year, it hugs the horizon — where extra atmosphere often turns it amber or gold and the 'moon illusion' makes it look strikingly large.
What's the difference between the Buck Moon and the Strawberry Moon?
They're consecutive summer full moons. The Strawberry Moon is June's full moon and the lowest of the year; the Buck Moon is the next one, July's, riding almost as low. Both can look large and golden at moonrise because they sit close to the horizon.
