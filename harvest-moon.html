The Harvest Moon

The full moon closest to the September equinox — the one that rises near sunset for several nights running, lighting the fields long enough to bring in the harvest.

Short answer
The Harvest Moon is the full moon closest to the September equinox — defined by astronomy, not the calendar, so usually September but sometimes October. In 2026 it falls September 26. Its signature trick: it rises only ~20 minutes later each night for several nights, putting a bright moon in the early-evening sky right at harvest time.

Why farmers named it

Most nights, the Moon rises about 50 minutes later than the night before. But around the September equinox, the Moon's path makes a shallow angle with the horizon, so it rises only 20–25 minutes later each evening. For several nights in a row a nearly-full Moon appears soon after sunset — giving pre-electric farmers crucial extra light to keep harvesting after dark. That practical gift is the whole reason for the name.

Frequently asked questions

Is the Harvest Moon bigger than other full moons?
No — it's the same size. It just rises around sunset, so people catch it low on the horizon where the 'moon illusion' makes it look enormous and its colour turns warm orange. Those are perception and atmosphere effects, not a real size change.
What comes after the Harvest Moon?
The Hunter's Moon — the next full moon after the Harvest Moon, usually in October. It shares the same early-evening rising effect, historically useful for hunting by moonlight after the fields were cleared.
